Cafe Coffee Day (CCD) Case Study INTRO / HOOK At one time it was the “India’s biggest coffee chain… More than 1700 outlets… A brand that changed India’s café culture forever… Today, let’s explore the rise and fall of Cafe Coffee Day… also known as CCD.” THE BEGINNING “Cafe Coffee Day was founded by V.G. Siddhartha in 1996 under Coffee Day Enterprises Limited. At a time when café culture was still new in India, Siddhartha introduced a modern coffee experience for young Indians. The first CCD outlet opened in Bengaluru, and slowly it became a favorite hangout spot for students, couples, friends, and entrepreneurs.” RISE OF CCD “During the 2000s, CCD expanded aggressively across India. The company opened cafés in malls, highways, airports, colleges, and major cities. Soon, CCD became India’s largest coffee chain with over 1700 outlets across 141 cities. The brand also expanded internationally into countries like: Malaysia, Nepal, Austria, Czech Republic, and Egypt. But CCD was not just a café business. It changed the way young Indians socialized. It became a favorite meeting place for students, couples, entrepreneurs, startup founders, and friends. For many people in the 2000s, visiting CCD was not only about drinking coffee — it was about spending quality time and experiencing modern urban culture. Its famous slogan — ‘A lot can happen over coffee’ became part of Indian pop culture. BUSINESS MODEL “One of CCD’s biggest strengths was its business model. The company: • Owned coffee plantations • Processed coffee beans • Supplied coffee directly to its cafés This reduced dependency on suppliers and increased profit margins. CCD also earned revenue through: • Food items • Beverages • Merchandise • Coffee vending machines For many years, the model looked highly successful.” Then the PROBLEMS BEGIN “But rapid growth came with a hidden danger — debt. CCD borrowed huge amounts of money for: • Real estate • Business expansion • Investments At the same time, competition increased. International brands like Starbucks entered India. Food delivery apps and new café chains changed customer behavior. Operating costs increased, profits reduced, and many outlets became unprofitable. Slowly, the company started facing financial pressure.” TRAGIC TURNING POINT “In July 2019, the company faced its biggest shock. V.G. Siddhartha went missing and was later found dead near the Nethravathi River in Karnataka. A letter written by Siddhartha to the board of directors and employees became public. In the letter, he reportedly took responsibility for not building a sufficiently profitable business model The incident shocked the entire Indian business community.” MALAVIKA HEGDE’S COMEBACK “After Siddhartha’s death, his wife, Malavika Hegde, took responsibility for reviving the company. At that time, CCD had nearly ₹7000 crore debt. Through strong leadership and determination, she reduced debt, sold assets, closed unprofitable outlets, and focused on core operations. Under her leadership, the company managed to reduce its debt significantly from around ₹7,000 crore to nearly ₹500 crore. Her efforts were widely appreciated because she not only worked to save a business but also protected the livelihood of thousands of employees connected with CCD. Malavika Hegde became an example of resilience, calm leadership, and determination during a crisis. Her work played a major role in stabilizing the company and rebuilding confidence among investors, employees, and customers. ENDING / CONCLUSION “Cafe Coffee Day was not just a café chain. It transformed India’s urban culture and inspired an entire generation of entrepreneurs. Because sometimes… a company becomes more than a business. For millions of Indians… it became an emotion.”
